The Post-Sale Wasted Spend Effect

What happened to efficiency in the two weeks after the 15 June sale ended — across Google Ads (all campaigns) and the Meta Conversions campaign. Season window 18 May – 8 July 2026.

When a big sale ends, conversion rates fall back to normal almost overnight — but automated bidding and budgets don't. For a week or two the machines keep buying as if the sale-week conversion rate still held, so you pay well above target for each booking. This is the post-sale overbidding pattern. It cost Google Ads roughly A$4,242 in Winter 26. On Meta the same directional dip appeared, but the dollar damage was far smaller once budget was trimmed.

Google Ads Primary story · A$4,242

All campaigns excluding Demand Gen TOF. Weeks run Monday–Sunday.

The sale week (15 Jun) did exactly what a sale should: conversion rate jumped to 9.0% and spend scaled hard. The problem is what happened next. In the week of 22 June, Smart Bidding held the cost-per-click at its season peak (A$1.75, actually the highest of the whole season) — but the conversion rate had already collapsed from 9.0% back to 5.9%. Same expensive clicks, far fewer of them converting. Cost per conversion blew out from a ~A$19 baseline to A$29.93, then A$31.56 the following week.

The mechanism, in one line: post-sale, the bids stayed high while the conversion rate halved. Google kept paying sale-week prices for clicks that no longer converted like sale-week clicks.

A subtle tell: Search impression share actually fell post-sale (31.4% → 23.8% → 21.3%). So the waste wasn't from buying more of the auction — it was from paying peak prices for a shrinking share of clicks that converted poorly.

Meta — Conversions campaign Parallel effect · much milder

codecamp-winter26-conversions. Result = Website purchases (bookings). Benchmark = A$33 target cost per result.

The same directional dip is clearly there: the sale week (15 Jun) was the campaign's most efficient of the season at A$24.19 per booking with a booking rate of 5.2%, and the week after (22 Jun) that reverted to A$35.08 as the booking rate halved to 3.5% — all while spend was pushed to its season peak of ~A$4,000. That is the post-sale reversion.

But the mechanism differs from Google, and it matters. On Meta, CPM and CPC actually eased post-sale (CPM A$18.07 → A$16.06 → A$14.95) — there was no bid inflation. The cost-per-booking rise was almost entirely conversion-rate decay: sale-primed demand evaporating. And critically, measured against the A$33 target you hold the campaign to, the post-sale damage was small: about A$237 of excess in the week of 22 June, which then self-corrected to under-target the following week once budget was trimmed.

Honest read: against the A$33 target, Meta's post-sale weeks weren't especially wasteful — the campaign came back under target by week 2. The larger over-target spend on Meta actually sat in the pre-sale ramp weeks (25 May, 1 Jun), not after the sale. Meta's post-sale exposure is an order of magnitude smaller than Google's.

What this means — and what to do next sale

Two channels, one root cause, different severity.

  • Google is where the money is lost. ~A$4,242 over two weeks, concentrated in the generic Search and PMax campaigns, driven by bids that stayed at peak while conversion rate halved. This recurs after every big sale.
  • Meta reverted too, but held its target. Cost per booking rose ~45% off the sale-week low, yet stayed close to the A$33 target and self-corrected by week 2 — because the lever there is budget, and budget was pulled back. No bid-inflation problem to fix.
  • The fix is pre-emptive, not reactive. The waste happened in the days before anyone would normally react. Because the effect persisted ~2 weeks, acting one week early protects roughly double the single-week loss.
Recommended play for the next sale:
  • Google: tighten target CPAs (or trim daily budgets) on the generic Search and PMax campaigns going into the week after the sale ends — don't wait for cost/conversion to blow out. Brand can be left alone; its overshoot is volume, not bid inefficiency.
  • Meta: step the Conversions budget down into the first post-sale week rather than holding it at sale-week peak. The week-2 correction shows this works; doing it a week earlier removes even the modest week-1 excess.
